Teaching Philosophy

I cannot imagine my life without music. Since I was five years old, I wanted to play the violin. I lived in a small town in North Dakota that did not have a string program (the Suzuki method was only in Japan at that time). At age 5, I started piano lessons and at 10 my family moved to the capitol of North Dakota and I was able to start the violin in an excellent public-school program. My early training was “traditional”, which was before the advent of the Suzuki Method. After college I discovered the benefits of the Suzuki Method and became a certified Suzuki Instructor, books 1-10.

Numerous studies show that students that study music, test higher on standardized tests, are more successful in school, and are generally more well-rounded individuals. Not everyone will have a career in music, but we all can become educated in music and experience the joy of music in our lives.

I have taught violin to all ages and levels. When starting young students (ages 4 through school age), I prefer the Suzuki method because of the benefits of early success (see “The Suzuki Method” section). I have students that are new beginners and students with previous experience that need additional training.

It is important for each of my students to have an individualized curriculum and I support them in their personal music goals. Lessons can include any music a student plays in public, such as auditions, chair tryouts, and orchestra music. It is important that my students have a nurturing and positive environment at their lessons. I have taught many students over multiple years and the result is a special student/instructor relationship.

Music feeds my soul and I look forward to sharing with you my passion for violin.
